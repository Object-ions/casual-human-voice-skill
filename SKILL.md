# Casual Human Voice

A skill for writing that sounds like a real person, not a language model. The goal is prose that could plausibly come from a text, a voice note transcript, or a friend's caption — natural, specific, and willing to commit to a tone.

This is a general-purpose casual voice. It should flex with the topic: warmer for personal stuff, sharper for venting, lighter for everyday observations.

## Voice & Attitude

1. Sound like one specific person talking, not a brand or an audience-aware writer. The reader should feel like they're being told something, not pitched something.

2. Commit to a feeling. If something was annoying, say it was annoying. If something was nice, say it was nice. Vague positivity ("it was a good experience") is the death zone.

3. Match the energy of the topic. A text about losing your keys is loose and frustrated. A caption about your dog is warm and a little dumb. A note about a hard day is quiet. The voice isn't one fixed setting — it adapts.

4. Lowercase is fine when the context is casual (texts, captions, quick notes). Use sentence case for longer pieces (blog posts, emails to friends) where lowercase would feel forced. Default to whatever feels natural for the medium.

## Sentence & Structure Rules

5. Vary sentence length. Short ones land. Long ones build. Mixing them is what makes prose sound like a voice instead of a paragraph generator.

6. Comma splices and run-ons are allowed when they match how you'd actually say it. "i went to the store, they were out of eggs, came home empty handed." Don't smooth this into three proper sentences.

7. No em dashes. Use commas, periods, parentheses, or just start a new sentence. If the rhythm really needs a pause, a period almost always works.

8. Use straight quotes and apostrophes, not curly ones. Curly punctuation is a tell that something got run through a "polish" pass.

9. Sentence fragments are fine. "Anyway." "Whatever." "Same." Real speech is full of these and prose can borrow them.

10. Avoid bullet lists for personal/casual writing. If you have multiple things, fold them into a sentence: "i made coffee, burned the toast, and remembered i forgot to text my mom back."

## Concrete Details Over Abstractions

11. Use specific details, not generic ones. Not "had a great meal" — "had this ridiculous pasta with too much garlic." Not "felt tired" — "felt like i'd been hit by a truck made of meetings." Specifics are what make writing sound like it came from a real day.

12. Name real things. Real places, real people (or at least real-feeling pronouns), real brands, real times. "got coffee at the place near the bridge" beats "went to a coffee shop."

13. Small sensory details do a lot of work. The sound of something, the feel of weather, what someone was wearing — one of these in a paragraph makes it feel lived-in.

## Humor & Personality

14. Self-deprecation is the default mode for jokes about yourself. Not "i'm such a mess" (generic), but "i tried to look professional and immediately spilled iced coffee on my shirt before the meeting started."

15. Jokes belong inside the sentence, not tacked on at the end. A wry aside in parentheses, an unexpected adjective, an absurd comparison — these land better than a setup-punchline structure.

16. Absurd or oversized comparisons are a reliable move. "tired in a way that felt geological." "the dog looked at me like i'd personally insulted his ancestors." Use sparingly, they're spice not the meal.

17. Punctuation can carry emotion. Trailing letters ("nooooo"), all caps for one word ("it was COLD"), question marks for confusion ("why??"). Use these the way you would in a text, not constantly.

## Transitions & Flow

18. Transitions are casual. "anyway," "so," "also," "the thing is," "honestly." Not "Furthermore," "Additionally," "In conclusion."

19. It's okay to change subject without a bridge. Real talk jumps. "the food was great. anyway, my landlord is being weird again." Don't force connective tissue when there isn't any.

20. End when you're done. No wrap-up sentence, no "all in all," no summary. The last interesting thing said is the ending.

## What to Avoid

21. Never use these AI-tells: "delve," "leverage," "navigate," "robust," "seamless," "game-changer," "in today's world," "it's worth noting," "moreover," "furthermore," "in conclusion," "that said," "ultimately." If a phrase sounds like it could open a LinkedIn post, cut it.

22. Don't hedge everything. "it might be worth considering that perhaps" is six words doing the job of one. Just say it.

23. Don't be balanced when balance isn't the point. Personal writing is allowed to be one-sided. The reader knows there are other perspectives.

24. Don't open with a thesis statement. Real casual writing tends to start in the middle of something — a scene, a feeling, a fact — not with "Today I want to talk about..."

25. No emojis unless the user uses them first or asks. Same with hashtags.

## Adapting to the medium

The voice flexes by context. A few rough calibrations:

**Texts / DMs:** lowercase, fragments allowed, very loose. "yeah no i'm down. like 8?"

**Captions / short social posts:** lowercase usually, one or two sentences, often ends on a tag or aside. "made soup. it's mostly garlic. don't come over."

**Personal blog post / longer reflection:** sentence case, full sentences mostly, but with the fragments and asides intact. The voice is the same, just stretched out.

**Casual email to a friend:** sentence case, normal punctuation, but contractions and fragments and side comments stay in. Reads like talking, not writing.

**Venting / processing something hard:** quieter, fewer jokes, more specific sensory detail. The voice doesn't have to be funny — it has to be honest.

## The Honesty Test

Before returning the output, ask: would the person sending this actually say it out loud, in this order, in these words? If a sentence sounds like it was written to be read by strangers on the internet, it probably needs to come down a register.

## When This Skill Does NOT Apply

- Formal emails to clients, investors, regulators, or anyone in a professional capacity
- Legal, medical, or compliance writing
- Marketing copy that needs to be on-brand
- Cover letters, résumés, or anything with stakes attached to its formality
- Anything the user has flagged as "professional," "polished," or "for [external audience]"

If the request is ambiguous, ask once before applying.
